*This is the third post in the series "We made a map of the doom debate," produced as part of the AI Safety Camp 2026 "Assumptions of the Doom Debate" project. The [first post](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/dZZD5Lviid2mALAZL/we-made-a-map-of-the-doom-debate) introduces the tree and its resources; the [second](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/AZrMGwBPMJmAcgqX8/we-made-a-map-of-the-doom-debate-here-s-how-the-breakdown) walks the nodes and explains how the breakdown works. This post maps the real-world viewpoints onto that tree. The interactive tree is [here](https://lifeuniversesafety.com/doom-assumptions/index.html).*

TL;DR:

- The AI-risk debate mixes two kinds of question: empirical ones evidence can settle, and framing choices which evidence cannot settle. These framing choices muddy the discussion.
- We place the prominent figures and viewpoints of the AI debate on the previously described tree.
- Grouping viewpoints based on failure mode, we see where viewpoints actually overlap, but differ on probability, prescription, or definition of doom.
- We also note the tree is lopsided when populated with real viewpoints, with most voices piling onto a few branches, and with some nodes mostly empty.

## Why this map exists

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** Opens at the tree (the two-kinds-of-question thesis now lives in the TL;DR). The reading rule — sort by mechanism — and the payoff. Emphasis stays sparing.
:::

The first two posts in this series focus on how we approached the problem of decomposing the AI risk debate. Does powerful AI raise or lower risks? Does failure come from one system or many? Does the system expect the result? We sort failure modes by their broadest characteristics. Crucially, we separate framing questions to focus on actual cruxes, decision points with apparent empirical answers, through which the AI debate actually happens.

Sorting viewpoints by this way helps shine a light on where true disagreements arise. In some cases, otherwise disparate views may appear on the same node but argue over the likelihood or timeframe of failure, or over humanity's ability to take control of the situation. This is also helpful data.

As the last post, we will step through our tree, this time placing prominent figures on it. These are our best approximations, based on past public comments and writing.

## II. AI-Driven Pathway

Worlds where AI raises the danger, in one of two shapes: many systems at once, or a single dominant one. While it is not mutually exclusive to expect risks from both single optimizers and competing systems, we categorize here by a viewpoint's more likely failure mode.

{{< include ../chapters/threat-models-v2/b-distributed.qmd >}}

### II.2 Single dominant AI pathway

One system carries the danger. It splits by whether that system models what it is doing, and, if so, whether it expects the result.

#### II.2.a Internal model pathway

The system under this node knows what it is doing when it raises risks. This includes, for example, a system that willingly accepts a 5% chance of catastrophe. The branch is subdivided on whether the system expects its actions to cause harm.

## I. Non-AI Pathway

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** The skeptics. Not a threat model but the bet that none fire — three distinct reasons behind one low number. LeCun's real worry (concentration) is off-tree. Links: LeCun only.
:::

This branch focuses on pathways where AI development decreases, or at least does not increase, existential risk. Our top optimist figure is LeCun, who bases his argument on architectural limitations. He bets that large language models are [token-interpolators without the world-models, memory, or planning](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=BZ5a1r-kVsf) the dangerous scenarios require, so the dangerous system never arrives. He has called the extinction worry ["complete B.S."](https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/12/metas-yann-lecun-says-worries-about-a-i-s-existential-threat-are-complete-b-s/) His bet is that the risk never materializes in the first place and we could call him a skeptic.

Alternative flavors of viewpoints on this node argue that dangerous systems arrive but remain controllable, or that ASI does arrive and is inherently good.

########## chapters/threat-models-v2/b-distributed.qmd ##########
### II.1 Multipolar AI pathway

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** Cut hard. Two stories (racing / gradual disempowerment) that differ mainly on evidentiary frame. Critch is the number-hides-mechanism case (~35% loss-of-control + ~50% dehumanization). Toner/Russell/Ord demoted to a side note — Russell really belongs at Architectural Agency, Ord is a time-horizon figure. Links: Carlsmith, Gradual Disempowerment, Critch.
:::

Assuming powerful AI does raise existential risk, the multipolar pathway covers threats that arise from many systems interacting. It is one of the more popular nodes, serving to collect many of the real-world risks and implementations of even weak AGI. The clearest example is racing that destabilizes global security. What lives here are the racing failures that never produce a single strong optimizer, but still elevate global tensions, likely by incorporating AI too hastily into military or economic decisions.

A failure mode here is gradual disempowerment, where capable systems, not necessarily misaligned, accrete economic and political power until human control becomes a formality, with no single villain and no sharp takeover. Carlsmith makes this argument in [his work on power-seeking AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.13353), and the [Gradual Disempowerment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16946) paper develops the same idea, tracing how humans could be sidelined incrementally as automated systems take over economic and political functions.

Andrew Critch sits here too. He has [estimated an 85% p(doom)](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Kobbt3nQgv3yn29pr/my-motivation-and-theory-of-change-for-working-in-ai) but expects us to "dodge" a fast single takeover, only to succumb to a slower gradual disempowerment that sees humans outcompeted and sidelined by an economy that no longer needs them. He [splits his p(doom) between loss of control and economic disempowerment](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Kobbt3nQgv3yn29pr/my-motivation-and-theory-of-change-for-working-in-ai).

########## chapters/threat-models-v2/a-knowing-optimizer.qmd ##########
##### II.2.a.a AI expects catastrophe

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** The canonical, crowded leaf. The Sharp Left Turn (Yudkowsky/Soares, prior-dominant, first-critical-try) and Architectural Agency (Bengio, Russell) share it and split on the agency crux, a prescription difference. Links trimmed to four.
:::

This is the canonical AI failure mode: a system that is capable, understands it is increasing risks, and does so anyway.

The most popular and controversial failure mode here is the [Sharp Left Turn](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/uMQ3cqWDPHhjtiesc/agi-ruin-a-list-of-lethalities). Yudkowsky and Soares argue that any capable optimizer develops power-seeking subgoals in an emergent way, on a compressed timeline, which makes iterative testing insufficient. Their case mostly rests on priors, and they believe a hard stop is the only real solution. Recent empirical work points the same way. In December 2024 Anthropic and Redwood documented [alignment faking](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.14093), and Apollo found models scheming in context when it served a goal. Most viewpoints concerned with instrumental convergence or orthogonality will end up on this node.

We also place Bengio here, but make an important distinction. While the former are strongly pessimistic, Bengio believes dangerous agency is a separable choice, and is investigating a non-agentic [Scientist AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.15657). Note that the threat models here are essentially the same, with different opinions on whether it is ultimately solvable. Stuart Russell also belongs here for similar reasons. His [CIRL](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.03137) work shares the failure mode but differs on the prescription, building deference to humans into the objective.

########## chapters/threat-models-v2/c-the-mistake.qmd ##########
##### II.2.a.b AI does not expect

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** Most contestable pin. The node is competent pursuit of a misspecified goal, not betrayal. Christiano placed by tractability + evidence, and he is the one genuine straddle (his "bang" edges to the leaf above); Amodei placed by mechanism, his race-logic decision-context. *Sleeper Agents* is strictly deception, so it backs the broad "methods are unreliable" claim, not this node specifically.
:::

This node describes failure modes where the system understands catastrophe but is not actively working towards it. For example, Anthropic's *Sleeper Agents* showed [standard safety training failing to remove hidden misbehavior](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.05566). This viewpoint does not seem especially popular, possibly because models with a complete world model would also well understand its own risks.


########## chapters/threat-models-v2/d-unwitting.qmd ##########
#### II.2.b No internal model

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** Empty branch, a finding, not a portrait. Once Christiano leans toward "expects," the single-dominant branch concentrates at the knowing-optimizer leaf, leaving this and the "mistake" node thin.
:::

The tree also acknowledges another mostly unoccupied branch, where a capable system causes catastrophe without an internal model of it. In [Post 2](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/AZrMGwBPMJmAcgqX8/we-made-a-map-of-the-doom-debate-here-s-how-the-breakdown) we used the example of an oblivious parent who gives the wrong medicine, with all the power to cause harm and none of the understanding to avoid it. However, this is not a widely held concern. We believe this is because it is less flashy and less sharp than the other failures, and because many tacitly assume anything capable enough to dominate would not be that ignorant of its own effects.

## Appendix: The Cruxes

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** The factual bets the gallery pointed to, as a reference glossary. Warning shots dropped (it was the evidentiary axis applied to early signs, and the Christiano/Yudkowsky contrast in "What the layout reveals" already carries it). The transition map removed.
:::

The factual bets the gallery kept pointing to, collected here. Each could in principle be settled, which is what separates a crux from a framing choice, where there is nothing to settle. Settle one and a thinker moves from one position to another.

| Crux | What hangs on it | Who it separates |
|---|---|---|
| Agency, emergent or chosen? | If dangerous agency comes free with capability, architectural avoidance is wishful thinking. If it is a separable design choice, Bengio's agenda is coherent and the build path matters. | Sharp Left Turn vs. Architectural Agency |
| Oversight, tractable in time? | Whether institutions can align advanced capability before deploying it, or whether the first real failure is already too late. | Sharp Left Turn vs. Control Failure |
| Arrival, does the dangerous system come and how fast? | Whether scaling current architectures reaches the dangerous regime at all, and whether it arrives sharply or as a slow ramp. Fills or empties the dangerous branches. | the skeptics vs. the gallery, and fast vs. slow within it |
| Coordination, can actors hold a line together? | Whether durable governance can change competitive incentives, or whether racing is structurally unavoidable even among well-meaning labs. | Multipolar Racing vs. those who see governance as a real lever |

## Conclusion

::: {.aside .meta-note}
**META:** One tight conclusion. The diagnostic, the honest caveat folded to two sentences, the next-post pointer. No recap of the gallery.
:::

This map was built to help locate disagreements. We separate out framing choices and identify core crux questions where most disagreements lie. We also try to represent different sources of risk under a unified framework. We find that, even after accounting for differences in framing and within the same failure node, there is still room to disagree about prescription and likelihood.

The next and final post will focus specifically on optimization, since this is one of the central cruxes between those who expect dangerous agency to emerge from optimization and those who believe it can be designed out.
